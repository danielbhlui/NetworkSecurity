Marriott data breach
a tale about IT security, corporate greed, and Chinese espionage.

Presentation by Daniel Bing Hung LUI

 

Background information

The hack on Starwood is believed to begin in 2014, no ransom money was ever asked by the hackers.

Marriott hotels purchased the Starwood chains of hotels in 2016 and fired most of Starwood¡¦s corporate staff, including those responsible for information technology in Starwood, in order to generate ¡§synergy¡¨ (cost savings).

Marriott purchased Starwood in 2016, but nearly two years later, the former Starwood hotels hadn¡¦t been migrated to Marriott¡¦s own reservation system and were still using IT infrastructure inherited from Starwood.

The hack is discovered in late 2018 by Mariott.

 

What types of data were affected?

Hundreds of millions of people had their passport and credit card numbers stolen, which could have disastrous personal impacts.

The credit card number aspects are particularly worrying, and were made possible by yet another security failing on Marriott¡¦s part: while the credit card numbers were stored in encrypted form, the encryption keys were stored on the same server, and were also apparently scooped up in the breach.

As for the passport numbers, while some were encrypted, the majority were simply saved in the clear.

 

What happened?

It is reasonable to guess that Remote Access Trojan (RAT) and Mimi Katz, a tool for sniffing out username/passwood combos in system memory, have given the attackers the control of the administrator account.

A series of cultural and business factors are the root cause of the breach. The layoff is Starwood¡¦s staff (including IT staff) to create ¡§synergy¡¨ and higher profits that drive these sorts of mergers is to blame. And also, Marriott was nowhere close to ready to book guests at its thousands of newly acquired hotels with its own in-house reservation system, and so Starwood¡¦s old system limped on, zombie-like, infected with malware, breached by hackers, and without much by way of continuity of care, for another two years before the breach was finally discovered. Another root cause.

 

Who was responsible?

State sponsored hackers employed by the Chinese intelligence services are responsible, according the NY Times, citing sources in the US government.

The code and attack patterns used match up with techniques employed by state-sponsored Chinese hackers; the attackers used a cloud-hosting space frequently used by Chinese hackers.

Another clue that this breach is part of a government attack rather than mere cybercriminals is the fact that none of those millions of valuable records have ended up for sale on the dark web.

 

What was the motivation for the attack?

The government sources speculate that it was part of a broader Chinese effort to acquire massive amounts of data on American government employees and intelligence officers; Marriott is the top hotel provider for the U.S. government and military. The stolen passport numbers in particular could be used to track movements around the world.

The larger goal is to create a data lake of information on American government employees and agents that big data techniques can be used to analyze.

 

Were the escalation stopped ¡V how?

Yes, the hotel chain discovered the hack in progress and called in the experts, after the hack has been going on for years.

 

Was the Business Continuity Plan instigated?

No, BCP was not instigated as there was no ransome demand.

 

Were affected individuals notified?

Yes, they are notified and compensated to a small extent, but perhaps because there seems to be no immediate threat of the stolen data being used for conventional fraud, Marriott has not gone out of its way to compensate any of its customers whose data was stolen. The New York Times quotes a Marriott spokesperson as saying the company would pay the replacement cost for a passport with a new number or cover credit card expenses ¡§if fraud has taken place.¡¨ While the potential damage from personal data now stored with Chinese intelligence is in theory profound, it¡¦s difficult to quantify, especially for individuals.

 

What were the social, legal and ethical implications of the decisions made?

Marriott and Starwood customers aren¡¦t taking the matter lying down. Multiple class action lawsuits have already been filed, and the failure of Marriott to perform due diligence on Starwood¡¦s information security (or lack thereof) has been specifically singled out in the court documents from the plaintiffs. Accenture, the consulting company to whom Starwood (and subsequently Marriott) had outsourced much of its day-to-day IT operations, is also being sued as part of the same lawsuit.

Starwood and Marriott were guilty of basic security failings: Lack of defense in depth that allowed attackers to stay in the system for years after breaching it, for instance, and failure to keep encrypted data and the keys used to encrypt it separate. Marriott failed to follow the most important cybersecurity rule: assume you are compromised and act accordingly.

Unit 06 Seminar

Generative AI in Cybersecurity

Presentation By Chih Ming Lee, Daniel Bing Hung Lui, Salem Alnuaimi

 

Generative AI in Cybersecurity?

Generative AI represents a transformative shift in how security professionals predict, detect, and respond to threats.

It uses generative adversarial networks (GANs), to simulate cyberattacks and defensive strategies.

The capability of generative AI allows cybersecurity systems to evolve rapidly, adapting to new threats much faster than the traditional detection methods would.

 

Using Generative AI in Cybersecurity

Gen AI can be used by both perpetrators of cybercrime and those preventing and mitigating the risk of cybercrime.

Gen AI is used in Cybersecurity to create new fake data that can be used to train machine learning models to detect cyber attacks. These models can then be used to identify and prevent future attacks.

In security operations centers (SOCs), AI models can identify patterns of cyber threats, such as malware, ransomware, or unusual network traffic.

Gen AI can create more sophisticated data analysis and anomaly detection in security event and incident management (SIEM) systems.

 

Enhancing Threat Detection and Response

Gen AI can create sophisticated models that predict and identify unusual patterns indicative of cyber threats, much faster and more effectively than traditional methods.

Continuous learning means adapting to new and evolving threats, ensuring that detection mechanisms are always several steps ahead of potential attackers.

Detailed insights into threat vectors and attack strategies means targeted responses and strengthened defense mechanisms against future attacks.

This interaction between detection and response fortifies cybersecurity frameworks, making them resilient against the increasingly sophisticated landscape of cyber threats.

 

Automating Security Measures

Gen AI streamlines cybersecurity by automating routine security tasks, such as configuring firewalls or scanning for vulnerabilities.

Gen AI  also customizes security protocols by analyzing vast amounts of data to predict and enforce the most effective measures for each unique threat scenario.

Human errors in identifying potential threats are reduced (improved accuracy).

Gen AI frees human resources for more complex issues.
