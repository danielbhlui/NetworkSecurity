Summary Post #2 (Unit 6)

With regards to the vulnerability in the Log4j 2 library, this student understands that ingenious unethical hackers are very, very determined and they will come up with novel approaches to hack. It is up to the rest of us to stop such hackers.

Existing strategies for logging

Signature-based detection uses a set of rules in order to determine which patterns of logs (derived from past or known attack patterns) should be flagged. This strategy only works if matches exactly with the attacks from past and would fail if attack were even slightly deviated from pattern. (Svacina and others, 2020)

Anomaly-based detection works better but it generates numerous false positive flags. Machine learning is usually used in such strategy. (Svacina and others, 2020)

Clustering is a branch of machine learning that focuses on log analysis and is used to group log statements together to compare clusters with each other to look for anomalies. (Svacina and others, 2020)

Future directions for research for logging

One problem with logs is they are post occurrence (therefore, after the facts of hacks and other anomalies). Research suggests that a possible future direction is to convert tools and methods to real-time analysis. Note that the approach of Ekelhart et al (2019) represents a sort of real-time (as opposed to post occurrence), clustering algorithm to perform log analysis. (Svacina and others, 2020)

Another research direction is to make the results of log analysis more efficient or accurate.  ¡§Multi-source log analysis then becomes necessary to correctly identify anomalies and threats.¡¨ Also, the limitations of machine learning are being worked on as they are resource-intensive and slow. Moreover, standardization of different log formats is also being worked on. (Svacina and others, 2020)

Lastly, yet another direction is to look for other anomaly detection other than logs. (Svacina and others, 2020)

This student believes that logs are here to stay, but would be much improved.

Ekelhart, A., Kiesling, E. and Kurniawan, K., (2018). Taming the logs-Vocabularies for semantic security analysis. Procedia Computer Science, 137, pp.109-119.
